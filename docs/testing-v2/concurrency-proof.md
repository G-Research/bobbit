## Analysis: what the seam changed, and where the bottleneck is now

**Before the seam** each verification command step spawned a `cmd.exe`/Git-Bash process (uncounted by the ledger). Under N-way load these uncounted spawns oversubscribed the 24 cores and starved the heavy gateway-integration tests into their 60/90 s timeouts → 3-way was 0/9 at 570–697 s.

**After the seam** the 5 heaviest command-step specs spawn nothing; tier-1 integration no longer collapses under contention. 3-way wall dropped to ≤ 557 s and Σ stays ≤ 24. **The command-step CPU-exhaustion mechanism is eliminated.**

**The remaining barrier is the tier-2 browser tier.** Each concurrent `test:v2` runs a full Playwright browser tier (571 tests, ~296 s isolated, many Chromium processes) plus real gateways. At 3-way that pushes wall to ~555 s and produces intermittent Chromium-contention flakes; at 4-way it blows 600 s outright. A secondary, smaller contributor is a handful of real-timer / real-git tier-1 tests (`extension-host-session-event-bus`, `steer-reconnect`, `multi-repo-goal`) that are timing-sensitive under CPU saturation.

## Recommendation (for the follow-up, not this seam)

To reach 0-flake at 3-way and unlock 4–5 way, target the **tier-2 browser tier** next (it is now the dominant cost), e.g.:
- reduce concurrent Chromium pressure (fewer playwright workers per run under load via the ledger's playwright split, or shard the browser tier), and/or
- de-flake the handful of real-timer/real-git tier-1 tests (drive on observable state, not wall-clock) or relocate the genuinely real-fidelity ones to the daily lane.

These are **out of scope for the command-step seam**, which has delivered its specific, measurable win.
